GoCue

A sound-cue controller for live shows — built in the spirit of QLab, Go Button, and Sound Cues. Build an ordered list of cues, then drive your show with one big GO button.

The idea in one paragraph

A show is an ordered cue list. One cue at a time sits on the standby playhead (the highlighted row). Pressing GO fires the standby cue — usually starting a sound — and advances the playhead to the next cue, ready for your next GO. That single, repeatable action is the whole interaction model: during a performance you mostly just press GO on time. Everything else is properties you set on cues beforehand.

Cue types

  • ♪Audio — plays a sound file with optional fades, looping, and level.
  • ■Stop / Fade — stops everything, or one targeted cue, optionally fading it out over a set time.
  • ⏱Wait — a no-op spacer. Combined with a pre-wait and auto-continue it inserts a timed gap into a sequence.

Cue properties

  • Volume — per-cue level, shown in dB (0 dB = unchanged, −∞ = silent).
  • Fade In / Fade Out — smooth ramps at the start and natural end of a sound.
  • Pre-wait — a delay between firing the cue and it actually executing.
  • Loop — repeat an audio cue indefinitely until stopped.
  • Auto-continue — when this cue fires, the next cue fires too. Chaining auto-continue with each cue's pre-wait lets a single GO trigger a whole timed sequence.

Transport & controls

  • GO — fire the standby cue (and any auto-continue chain), then advance. Space.
  • Stop All — instantly stop every playing cue and clear the active list. Esc.
  • Pause / Resume — freeze and resume all audio on the spot. P.
  • Master — a global level fader applied to everything.
  • Active Cues — a live panel showing what's playing, with progress, time remaining, and per-cue stop.
  • Move the playhead with ↑/↓, delete the selected cue with Delete.
  • Reorder cues by dragging the ⠿ grip handle (works with mouse and touch).

Your show is saved automatically

The entire show — every cue's settings and the audio files themselves — is stored locally in your browser. Reload the page and your show comes back exactly as you left it. The dot in the top-right shows save status (amber = saving, green = saved).

Everything stays on your device. Audio is decoded and stored locally; nothing is ever uploaded to a server. GoCue has no backend.

Works on phones & tablets

On small screens the layout adapts: GO and the transport move to a thumb-friendly bar at the bottom, the inspector and active-cues panel become a slide-up sheet (open it with the ▤ button or a cue's › edit affordance), and cue rows reflow to two lines.

Under the hood

GoCue is a single-page app with no framework and no build step — just modern browser APIs and native ES modules loaded straight from disk.

Web Audio API

All playback runs through an AudioContext. Each cue is an AudioBufferSourceNode → GainNode → master gain. Fades are sample-accurate scheduled gain ramps; pause uses suspend()/resume() so the clock — and every scheduled fade — freezes precisely.

IndexedDB

Persistence lives here: cue metadata in one store, the raw audio blobs in another, and app state (order, playhead, master) in a third. The whole thing is a local "show file" that survives reloads.

Architecture

The design follows John Ousterhout's A Philosophy of Software Design: a few deep modules with simple interfaces, complexity pulled down into them, and views kept loosely coupled by subscribing to events rather than being wired together.

// core src/util.js — pure helpers + a tiny event Emitter src/audio.js — AudioEngine: the Web Audio graph & fades src/store.js — Store: IndexedDB (falls back to a no-op store) src/show.js — Show: the model; orchestrates audio + storage, emits events // views (each subscribes to the model; none imports another) src/views/cue-list.js — list render, selection, drag reorder src/views/inspector.js — the cue property editor src/views/monitor.js — the live active-cues panel src/views/transport.js — GO/Stop/Pause, keyboard, file-drop, sheet src/app.js — composition root: builds and wires everything

Because views only listen for events like cues, current, or audio change, adding a new view — a clock, a waveform, MIDI triggers — is purely additive: a new module that subscribes, with no edits to the existing ones.
